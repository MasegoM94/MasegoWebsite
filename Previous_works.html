Final Design for Master's Data Visualisation project


2018

While I was doing my Masters, we had a course called Data Visualisation presented by Associate Professor Michelle Kuttel . Alternative, is the best way I can describe it. At first, I think most of the people in our class were confused at her approach. It was very different. She was trying to force us to become more creative and not be limited by technology. It was a huge adjustment as I see myself as an extreme left brainer. My degrees before that were very logical and analytical and although problem solving does require some sort of creativity, I mostly dealt with problem solving that required more logic than creativity. But Prof. Kuttel had a way of getting us to explore our creativity and honestly made us look at colour and objects differently. I have to thank her for that. With half of my course focusing on Data Science, we assumed that there would be coding involved in this course but Prof Kuttel didn't want us to be restricted by software. What has been attached here is an image of our final design for a project we had to do for the course. It looks at the language distribution in South Africa. Inspiration for this came from the cultural adjustment I had to make when moving from Gauteng to the Cape Town in the Western Cape. In Gauteng, one can generally speak any bantu language and the other would be able to understand even if it isn't their home language. However, in Cape Town the most dominant bantu language was Xhosa and there was always a "lost-in-translation" moment happening. So we were interested to see the distribution of these bantu languages across South Africa. Our aim was to create an infographic whilst trying to add as many elements of design, for example shapes, icons, patterns and colour, as possible without overwhelming the audience with information. I don't want to explain the design too much as the aim was for the audience to deduce the information we were trying to convey. We do assume that audience does have basic knowledge of South Africa's provinces and official Bantu languages.

Many thanks to Kuselo Ntsaluba as worked on this project together.

In the future I would like to try replicate this possibly in R or PowerBI
